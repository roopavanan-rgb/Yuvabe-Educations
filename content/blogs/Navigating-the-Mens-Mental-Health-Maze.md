Over the years, we’ve observed our men bearing the weight of societal expectations, a burden that compels them to stifle their need to express and convey their innate emotions. Whether this repression stems from the persistent intergenerationally enforced stigma, or the absurd notion of delicate masculinity, remains an open question, hanging tensely in the air.

From the early stages of life, boys are forcibly indoctrinated into the ideals of traditional masculinity—with traits like emotional restraint and absolute independence becoming the armour they don through life. This, as society has made us believe, is not their fault. The prevailing narrative paints vulnerability as a flaw, a weakness to be concealed, forcing men to bury their emotions beneath a façade of conformity. The toll? A substantial segment of the male population grappling and grudging silently through mental health challenges–their cries for support muffled by the weight of societal expectations.

![Toxic masculinity has created challenges for both men and women](/images/blogs/mmh-2.avif#lg-center)

The World Health Organization delivers a stark reality: global suicide rates consistently skew higher among men, with a staggering 75% of suicides attributed to them. This chilling statistic underscores the urgent need to confront mental health challenges specific to men, shining a poignant light on the severity of the issue.

Further amplifying this concern, a survey by the National Institute of Mental Health (NIMH) reveals that men are less inclined than women to seek mental health treatment. This trend takes root early on, as boys internalise the expectations tied to traditional masculinity, trading emotional openness for restraint and machismo. Consequently, a significant portion of the male population face mental health battles in isolation, denying themselves the support they desperately need.

In the 21st century, while the concept of mental health gains popularity, it remains a formidable taboo in many societies like ours. The deeply ingrained belief that discussing mental health issues equates weakness, coupled with the dread of judgment and societal pressure, compounds the struggles men encounter in addressing their well-being. Similar to physical wounds, mental wounds, when left untreated and in the dark, fester and grow, causing deeper, crippling issues down the line.

![Global suicide rates consistently skew higher among men, with a staggering 75% of suicides attributed to them.](/images/blogs/mmh-3.avif#md-left)

Society dictates that men present themselves a certain way, binding them in an emotional straitjacket that makes expressing vulnerability an exceptional challenge. So much so that seeking help would unwittingly brand them 'weak' or 'unmanly' with the sting of a searing rod.

The same study by NIMH uncovers a jarring fact: only 1 in 4 men with mental health issues seek professional help – a ratio which underscores the pressing need to reshape societal perceptions surrounding masculinity and mental well-being by injecting empathy into the narrative.

On a hopeful note, initiatives championing men's mental health awareness and programs fostering open conversations about mental health prove instrumental in encouraging men to speak out. Movements like Movember, specifically dedicated to raising awareness about men's health, carve pathways to increased understanding and funding for mental health initiatives, breaking down stereotypes one conversation at a time – that too, with as strong a symbol as the moustache! A long-standing emblem of overt masculinity.

![Young men of Yuvabe!](/images/blogs/mmh-4.avif#lg-center)

By embracing these methods, we roll up our sleeves and delve into the emotional landscape of the challenges men grapple with. Movements like Movember showcase the profound impact awareness campaigns can wield, breathing life into the possibility of positive change and a more compassionate understanding of what it is like to be a man in today’s world.
